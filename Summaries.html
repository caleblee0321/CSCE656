1. <2025-01-16 Thu>

1.1. Summary

The first chapter of Marshall McLuhan’s book states that the “medium is the message”. Medium is defined as an extension of ourselves, which is exemplified by the usage and production of machines. Contrarily, the electric light is used as an example of a medium without a message. Despite this, a point is made that activities reliant on the electric light become the content of the electric light to support that the medium is the message. McLuhan continues to emphasize medium of media in addition to the environmental and cultural contexts in which media operates, such as during the French Revolution or in the context of the English institution of Parliament. Media is also associated with changes in the proliferation and spread of culture, leading to “detribalization”. The medium is continuously stressed, as society tends to focus on the “content” over the invisible medium that gradually affects perception.

The second chapter categorizes hot and cold media based on the amount of information given to the listener. The “hotter” the media is, the more information is given to the listener and the less response input is required by the listener. When tied into different contexts, the disruptive impact of hot media in culture is exemplified during wars, or the introduction of new mediums fragmenting existing hierarchies in McLuhan’s example of Robert Theobald’s “The Rich and the Poor”. Over time, however, once hot media can cool down due to exhaustion. In the case of cold, numb, war, or hot, alarming situations, McLuhan considers humor and play to appropriately cool down culture. With the cooling of a medium, the third chapter discusses a reversal occurs in culture, where once hot mediums actually cool culture, like electricity decentralizing instead of centralizing civilization.

1.2. Learned

One thing I have learned is the categorization of hot and cold media to distinguish media that involves active or passive interaction with the listener. This is in contrast to my focus on the content dictating the hot or coldness of media consumed.

1.3. Uncertain

I am still uncertain of McLuhan’s statement that “medium is the message”. With emphasis on the medium itself, I can see some of the bigger picture cultural changes being influenced by a medium under the general definition of medium as an extension of ourselves, but I think McLuhan overgeneralizes his perspective of media. Though media does have significant impact on culture, it seems to overshadow historical events impacted by historical context and ideologies, which through McLuhan’s lens of media may be viewed as “content”.

2.1. Summary

The first chapter of Janet H. Murray’s Hamlet on the Holodeck introduces the reader to the holodeck from Star Trek, which Murray presents as a futuristic and utopian entertainment medium that is an evolution of older literary mediums offering similarly valuable experiences while remaining distinct from reality. The holodeck is contrasted with a technological medium in Aldous Huxley’s Brave New World, the “feelies”, which are a medium with no meaning and pure sensory pleasure. Along with the “feelies”, various other media portrayals present warnings of computer-based entertainment and equate it to drugs and addictive substances that confuse reality and the virtual world. Murray ponders the hopes and fears of advanced technological medium, but addresses how the medium becomes “transparent” as the storytelling itself becomes successful.

In the second chapter, multiform story structure is introduced and exemplified to describe its unique aspects of storytelling compared to linear stories. Multiform stories allow different points of view and self-awareness. As stories shifted toward electronic mediums, aspects of video games, the world wide web, and amusement rides are presented to describe how they allow for unique immersion as well as similarities to past mediums.

2.2. Learned

I have learned about the multiform structure of stories and the evolution of other older characteristics of storytelling such as hypertext referencing. The use of familiar examples shed light on certain structures and interactive aspects in story mediums that I was unaware of predating computer-based mediums.

2.3. Uncertain

Something I find uncertain yet interesting is the thinning barrier between writer/author and audience of a story in computer-based mediums.

16.1. Summary

The second chapter of “The Public Domain” begins with discussion of Thomas Jefferson’s views of the patent. Jefferson upholds the concept of intellectual property, but he keeps it distinct from natural rights as a way to encourage to innovation. Yet, it is a temporary exclusive right given by society if society deems necessary for the sake of encouraging and rewarding innovation. As with the Miranda Warning, Boyle describes the Jefferson Warning to highlight the monopolistic dangers of intellectual property rights, which was backed by Thomas Babington Macaulay. Another concern is the suppression of works by those who inherent copyrights because of one’s disagreements with another’s ideas. Some writers from the French Revolution such as Diderot argued that ideas should be treated like property rights, while others such as Condorcet argued that facts and ideas cannot be treated as permanent, personal privileges. Condorcet’s idea of limited privilege states that privilege applies only to an author’s name and expression over an idea or fact. On the other hand, radical views from those supporting mass education viewed intellectual property as tax on literacy or knowledge.

The third chapter compares and contrasts the “tragedy of the commons” and the enclosure of land with content in the abstract space of ideas and digital space, which Boyle describes as the second enclosure movement. There are unique differences in the space of information and ideas, as no use of a resource interferes with another’s use of the same resource. Compared to arable land information is considered pure and raw for innovation and generation of new information and ideas, making it difficult to promote efficiency of the market of information while also encouraging innovation. The success of the second enclosure was described as the “tragedy of the anticommons” on nontangible products and resources.

16.2. Learned

I learned about the Jefferson Warning, which I agree strongly with. I have thought of both extremes of having strong enforcement of intellectual property rights and none at all, and I agree that ideas should be contrained to the time and context of invention to encorage innovation without creating a monopoly to hold back others in the same field. I also learned about the treatment of intellectual property compared to physical property and differences in characteristics that bring challenges to balancing innovation and efficiency.

16.3. Uncertain

I have concern over the patenting of facts, but I am uncertain of how intellectual property would apply to the training and generated output of AI models. Though AI models are designed by the programmer or researcher, the input is often times someone else’s work, while the AI model will output a new piece of work mimicking aspects of the work it was trained on.
